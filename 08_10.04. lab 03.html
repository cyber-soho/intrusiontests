Overview & Objectives

Social engineering remains the most reliably effective attack vector in the real world. Technical defenses — firewalls, intrusion detection systems (IDS — software/hardware that monitors network traffic for suspicious activity), and endpoint protection — can all be bypassed with a convincing enough email. This lab places you in the role of an authorized red-team consultant hired to assess a fictional organization's human attack surface.

1.2 — The Psychology of Phishing

Before you can defend against social engineering, you must understand why human brains are vulnerable to it. This is not about blaming victims — it is about understanding psychological mechanisms that attackers deliberately exploit. The foundational model draws from Dr. Robert Cialdini's six principles of influence, adapted for the digital threat landscape.

01
Authority
People comply with requests from figures perceived as authoritative — a CEO, IT department, or government body bypasses critical thinking.
"As directed by the CISO (Chief Information Security Officer)..."
02
Urgency / Scarcity
Time pressure compresses the decision window. When people rush, scrutiny falls away.
"Your account will be suspended in 24 hours..."
03
Social Proof
People look to others to determine correct behavior. "Everyone else has already updated their credentials."
"All employees must complete verification by EOD."
04
Liking / Familiarity
People comply more readily with entities they recognize. Spear phishing (targeted, personalized phishing) exploits this through research.
Using the target's first name, job title, department.
05
Reciprocity
When someone does something for you, you feel obligated to return the favor. Attackers frame requests as follow-ups to benefits provided.
"Following up on the IT support ticket we resolved..."
06
Commitment
Once someone agrees to a small request, they are more likely to agree to larger ones — the "foot in the door" technique.
Benign survey before escalating to credential request.

3.1 — Anatomy of a Phishing Email

Every component of a phishing email serves a specific attacker goal — and every component has a corresponding red flag that a trained user should recognize.

ComponentAttacker's GoalRed Flag for Trained User
From AddressAppear legitimateDomain mismatch, extra characters, homograph characters
Reply-ToRedirect replies to attacker-controlled addressDifferent from From address — hover to compare
Subject LineCreate urgency or curiosityExcessive urgency, generic phrasing ("Urgent Action Required")
GreetingAppear personalized"Dear User" or "Dear Customer" instead of real name
Body TextBuild credibility, drive actionGrammar errors, unusual requests, wrong company terminology
Call to ActionGet click, download, or credential entryHover reveals wrong URL; button text doesn't match destination
FooterMimic legitimate communicationsWrong or missing unsubscribe link; wrong company address
AttachmentsDeliver payload or harvest credentialsUnexpected file types (.exe, .iso, .lnk, macro-enabled .docm)
Tracking PixelConfirm email was opened (1×1 invisible image)Not visible to user; revealed only in email source/headers

3.6 — Credential Harvesting Analysis (Theoretical)

Write a technical explanation (minimum 400 words) covering how credential harvesting works, why HTTPS (Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure) does not equal safety, how tools like Evilginx use a reverse-proxy technique to bypass MFA, how HSTS (HTTP Strict Transport Security — a browser security mechanism that forces HTTPS connections) provides partial defense, and how password managers detect domain mismatches.

🔍
Key Concept: HTTPS ≠ Safe Many users have been taught that the padlock icon (HTTPS) means a website is safe. This is a dangerous misconception. HTTPS only guarantees that the connection between your browser and the server is encrypted — it says nothing about whether the server itself is operated by a malicious actor. Phishing pages routinely use valid SSL/TLS certificates. Your explanation must clearly address this misconception and explain what users should check instead (domain name accuracy, not just padlock presence).

4.4 — Technical Controls (Defense-in-Depth)

Document how the following technical controls complement your awareness program. Show sample DNS (Domain Name System) records for the fictional meridian-fp.lab domain:

# SPF (Sender Policy Framework) — specifies authorized sending mail servers
meridian-fp.lab. IN TXT "v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all"

# DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) — cryptographic signature for tamper detection
default._domainkey.meridian-fp.lab. IN TXT "v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=MIGfMA0..."

# DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance)
# — policy: what to do when SPF/DKIM fail (none / quarantine / reject)
