The Paradigm Shift: Passive vs. Active Recon

CRITICAL AWARENESS: The moment you send a packet or make a call, the rules change. Testing without authorization is a criminal offence.

Passive Recon 👁️

  • ✓ Target cannot see your activities
  • ✓ Tools: WHOIS, DNS lookups, Shodan searches
  • ✓ Low legal risk
  • ✓ Objective: Gathering intelligence quietly

Active Recon ⚡

  • ⚠ Target CAN detect your probes
  • ⚠ Tools: Social engineering, active port scans
  • ⚠ High legal risk without signed RoE
  • ⚠ Objective: Engaging the target directly

Active Recon: The Human Vector

Social engineering manipulates human trust, authority, and social norms to gain unauthorized access. This section breaks down the psychology of manipulation, delivery mechanisms for payloads, and the critical importance of defensive verification protocols. Remember the pizza-box test: $30 of pepperoni can bypass a $50,000 access-control system.

🧠 Cialdini's Six Principles of Influence

Click a principle to view the attacker's application.

Doing a favour to ask for one back. E.g., holding a door, providing "free" IT help, then requesting a password.
Getting a small "yes" to lead to a big "yes". E.g., verifying a username first, before asking for the 2FA token.
"Everyone else already did it." E.g., "The rest of your department has already updated their credentials on this portal."
Impersonating IT, the CEO, or law enforcement. Targets are conditioned to comply with authority figures without questioning.
Building rapport, mirroring behaviour, finding common ground. People comply with people they like.
Creating artificial urgency. E.g., "Your account will be locked in 2 hours if you do not click here."

🛡️ E-mail Authentication & Defence

If all three pillars fail, the message is almost certainly a spoofed sender. Phishing defence requires both technical controls and user training.

SPF

Sender Policy Framework

Validates that the sender's IP address is officially authorized to send mail on behalf of the domain.

DKIM

DomainKeys Identified Mail

Applies a cryptographic signature to message headers, ensuring the email content was not tampered with in transit.

DMARC

Domain-based Message Auth...

The policy layer that combines SPF and DKIM, instructing the receiving server on what to do if an email fails authentication.

🔍 Payload Inspection: Magic Bytes

Vulnerability Analysis: The Machine Vector

Once the perimeter is established, we map the structural defenses and scan for technical weaknesses. This section covers the layers of Defense-in-Depth, the metrics used to calculate vulnerability severity (CVSS), and the realities of analyzing scanner outputs to find actual exploit chains.

🏰 Defence-in-Depth (DiD) Architecture

Every control fails eventually. Hover or tap layers to inspect example controls designed to catch the threat when the outer layer fails.

Layer 7: People & Policies
Awareness training, phishing sims, IR plans
Layer 6: Data Security
AES-256 at rest, TLS 1.3 in transit, DLP
Layer 5: Application Security
Input validation, SAST/DAST, OWASP Top 10
Layer 4: Host / Endpoint
EDR, anti-malware, OS hardening (CIS)
Layer 3: Network Segmentation
VLANs, ACLs, micro-segmentation, zero-trust
Layer 2: Perimeter / Edge
Next-gen firewall, IDS/IPS, WAF, DDoS mitigation
Layer 1: Physical Security
Locks, badges, CCTV, biometrics

📊 Scanner Noise: False Positives

Industry reality: A large percentage of vulnerability scanner findings require manual triage and suppression.

Top Causes of FP:

  • Banner-only detection (patches back-ported)
  • Version-range mismatches
  • Port-only inference (assuming service by port number)
  • Missing architectural context

🔗 Prioritisation & Exploit Chains

The CISO's nightmare: 200 vulnerabilities, limited resources. CVSS alone fails because individual medium-severity bugs can be chained to create critical kill-paths.

Example Kill-Path:

CVSS 5.0 Anon FTP
⬇️
CVSS 6.5 Credential Leak
⬇️
CVSS 7.2 SSH Login
⬇️
CVSS 7.8 Kernel LPE → Root Shell

Exploit Chain Analysis Method

  1. Model vulnerabilities as a directed graph.
  2. Run Depth-First Search (DFS) from each entry point to discover paths.
  3. Score chains by cumulative CVSS and final access level.
  4. Find the chain-breaking node: patch the vulnerability appearing in the most chains first.
